From FIFA to first team: how Tunde found his future

By the Sports Bridge team · 15 May 2026 · 4 min read

When sixteen-year-old Tunde first walked through the doors of a Sports Bridge community centre in Surulere, he wasn't looking for a football pitch. He was looking for a screen. The Bridge Gaming League had just launched, and word had spread fast: free, competitive FIFA, in a safe space, after school.

What he didn't know was that he'd just stepped onto the first plank of a much longer bridge.

Meeting young people where they are

For many young people in Lagos, the barrier to organised sport isn't talent or desire — it's access. Boots cost money. Pitches are far. And too often, no one is there to spot the spark. The Bridge Gaming League was built to solve a different problem first: how do you reach young people who've already drifted away from physical play?

The answer was to meet them on the platform they already loved. Within weeks, hundreds of teenagers were competing online and in person — and our coaches were right there alongside them, building trust one match at a time.

“I came to play FIFA after school. Now I captain my school team and I'm the first in my family with a scholarship. Sports Bridge didn't just give me a ball — they gave me a plan.”

Crossing the bridge

It was during a gaming session that one of our coaches noticed Tunde's competitive instinct and natural leadership. An invitation to a weekend taster session followed. Then a grassroots academy. Then, as his game developed, a place in our school league talent pool.

Eighteen months later, Tunde captains his school team, mentors younger players in the gaming league he came up through, and holds the foundation's first ever gaming-to-scholarship award — a place that keeps him in education while he develops on the pitch.

The returning circle

What makes Tunde's story matter most isn't where he's arrived — it's where he's pointed next. He now spends his Saturdays helping run the very gaming sessions that first brought him in, showing a new group of teenagers that the screen in their hands can be the start of something bigger.

That's the bridge, fully crossed — and then rebuilt for the next person to cross. It's the model in miniature, and it's why we do this work.

Stories like Tunde's are only possible because of our partners, volunteers and donors. If you'd like to help another young person find their bridge, there's a place for you on the team.
